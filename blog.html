MAORI BODY ART

Explore New ZealandMaori Culture

You might have heard about the Māori ‘tattoo’ culture, which is called Tā Moko and is seen as a cultural treasure.Tā moko is a unique expression of cultural heritage and identity through tattoos.Its genesis is in New Zealand.

Tā moko reflects an individual's whakapapa (ancestry) and personal history. In earlier times, it was an important signifier of social rank, knowledge, skill and eligibility to marry. Many of the design motifs are universal especially applied to the ihu,cheek,lower jaws and the curvilinear rays on the forehead and from the nose to mouth.The remaining elements were carefully chosen to accentuate and enhance the individual features, giving meaning to the expression Mataora, the living face.The main lines of Tā moko are called manawi ;heart, which represent an individuals life journey.Each moko was meant to make the wearer more fierce in battle and more attractive to the opposite sex. Receiving a moko is a big honor, and the only people without moko were slaves or workers. Because of their uniqueness, moko were also used in the 19th century to sign official documents; by drawing it in place of a signature.

The Art

]

At first Tā moko involved the use of broad toothed combs of varying widths called uhi,chisel blades. The blades were dipped in dark pigment, and struck into the skin with small mallets known as tā.Burnt wood was used to create black pigments whereas lighter pigments were derived from caterpillars infected with a certain type of fungus or burnt kauri gum mixed with animal fat.the art is a form of scarification similar to wood carving as deep furrow grooves were left on the skin.Traditionally, it is applied to the back, buttocks, legs and to the face. Because the head is considered the most sacred part of the body, a facial moko is the ultimate statement of the wearer’s identity. For women, a moko kauae, a moko on the chin is also very popular.

Evolution

In the early 19th century metals replaced bone in the manufacture of uhi,tattoing chisels and combs.Adoption of needle tattooing during late 19th century and early 20th century revolutionized Tā moko and until today is the most obiquitous method of tattooing used globally.

Despite being revitalised in recent timings by various artists and being both respected and accepted largely as a culture that should be conserved and preserved over generations to come,Tā moko faced a threat of extinction due to colonisation and adoption of Christianity in the mid 19th century.Missionaries and Pākehā(European New Zealanders) thought it distasteful even while being somewhat fascinated by it.Young men were more affected by this than women.
